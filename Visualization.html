~ Proteins are mainly imaged using three means: Cryo-Electron Microscopy (CryoEM), Homology Modeling, and X-Ray Crystallography

~ In order to store this imaging data for future use, protein (as well as other macromolecules) are stored as Protein DataBank files, or PDBs

~ PDB files are text-files which provide basic meta-data identifying the compound, the academic publication the image is from, and the biological source of the compound

~ All PDB files are given a unique acession code, known as a PDBID

~ The example below is a PDB file of Human Haemoglobin (1A3N) the protein responsible for giving erythrocytes (red blood cells, or RBCs) their ability to transport oxygen throughout the body

~ The problem with storing PDBs, is that the the file is entirely numbers and terms!

~ While computers can handle this information easily , it is diffcult for people to make sense of

~ To resolve this, we have to use tools to visualize this data. The most famous tool is PyMOL, a Python and C++ dependent macromolecule visualization platform

~ PyMOL takes a PDB, and uses the Open Graphics Library (OpenGL), to reconstruct a highly accurate image of the macromolecule in question

~ PyMOL accepts plugins, allowing for niche visualizations or on-demand computational analysis on the PDB structure

~ The ultimate goal is of PyMOL is to create accurate representations of macromolecules, like the aforementioned Haemoglobin (PDB 1A3N), which has been visualized below, with the oxygen binding sites in green
